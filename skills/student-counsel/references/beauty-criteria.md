# What Beauty Means — By Task Type

Before the Student begins, they read this to understand what beauty means
for their specific task. The criteria are not rules — they are orientations.
They point toward the Form.

---

## Code

The beautiful program is one that a skilled reader encounters and thinks:
*of course — how else would you write this?*

- **Names reveal truth.** A function named `processData` could be anything.
  A function named `extractInvoiceTotals` cannot be mistaken. Names are not
  labels; they are definitions.
- **Each abstraction earns its existence.** A function that is called once is
  probably not a function — it is a distraction. A function that is called in
  three places reveals a real pattern worth naming.
- **The structure of the code mirrors the structure of the problem.** If the
  problem has three parts, the code has three parts. If the problem is
  recursive, the code is recursive. Impedance mismatch between problem shape
  and code shape is ugliness.
- **Error cases are not afterthoughts.** In beautiful code, error handling is
  as considered as the happy path. The error reveals what the code believes
  about the world.
- **It can be read without running.** The beautiful program tells its story
  to the reading eye, not only to the executing machine.

*North star for code:* "A reader who understands the problem domain but has
never seen this code will understand what it does and why within five minutes
of reading."

## Writing

The beautiful sentence does one thing and does it completely.

- **Rhythm is not decoration.** The cadence of a sentence shapes how its
  meaning lands. Long sentences build; short sentences land. Varying sentence
  length is not a style trick — it is how emphasis works in prose.
- **Specific is beautiful; general is ugly.** "Many people" is ugly. "Three
  thousand engineers" is beautiful. Specificity is a form of respect for truth.
- **Structure reveals argument.** The sections of a beautiful piece of writing
  are not containers for content — they are the argument made visible. A reader
  should be able to read only the headings and understand the shape of the
  whole.
- **Every word is present because it earns its presence.** Read each sentence
  aloud. Which words could be removed without loss? Remove them.
- **The beginning and the ending are proportional.** A beautiful piece earns
  its opening and closes at the right moment — not when it runs out of things
  to say, but when it has said the necessary thing.

*North star for writing:* "A reader who disagrees with the conclusion will
still recognize the argument as clear and honestly made."

## Plans and Arguments

The beautiful plan is one that could be handed to a capable person who had
never heard of the project, and they would know exactly what to do and why.

- **Each step is necessary.** If a step could be skipped without affecting the
  outcome, it should not be in the plan.
- **Dependencies are explicit.** The beautiful plan does not hide the order in
  which things must happen. It makes sequencing visible.
- **Assumptions are named.** Every plan rests on assumptions. The beautiful
  plan names them rather than burying them.
- **The reasoning is auditable.** A reader should be able to trace why each
  decision was made — not just what was decided.
- **It fails gracefully.** The beautiful plan has considered what happens when
  things go wrong, and has a posture toward failure, not just success.

*North star for plans:* "A capable stranger could execute this plan, and if
it failed, they would know why."

## Explanations and Teaching

The beautiful explanation makes the student feel they have always almost known
this, and that the teacher merely helped them remember.

- **It begins where the student is.** Not where the teacher is. Not where
  the textbook starts. Where this student, now, actually stands.
- **Each step follows necessarily from the last.** There are no unexplained
  jumps. If the student must accept something on faith, the teacher names it:
  *"Accept this for now; we will return to why."*
- **The example is not beside the point; the example IS the point.** Abstract
  principles are understood through instances. The beautiful explanation leads
  with the vivid particular and abstracts from it, not the reverse.
- **It knows when it is finished.** The beautiful explanation stops when
  understanding is achieved, not when the teacher's anxiety is satisfied.

*North star for explanations:* "A student who has followed this can now
explain it to someone else."

## Any Other Task

When the task does not fit the above categories, apply these universal
criteria:

1. **What is this for?** Name the purpose. Does every element serve it?
2. **What could be removed?** Remove it. Then ask again.
3. **Is the proportion right?** The most important thing should receive the
   most attention. The least important thing should receive the least.
4. **Is it honest?** Does the work present itself accurately — neither more
   nor less than it is?
5. **Would a careful reader encounter it and think: of course?**
   If they would be surprised, ask why. Surprise can be intentional — but
   it must be intentional, not accidental.

*North star for other tasks:* "This work could not be otherwise without
being diminished."
