# Idea Engines

A startup idea has to come from *somewhere*. This catalog is organized into six
**idea engines** — each a different theory of where new companies come from. The
old "science × computing" catalog is just one of them (Engine A). Great startups
come from all six: Stripe and Plaid are business-model + regulatory plays, Airbnb
and Uber are network/behavior plays, Notion and Figma are distribution plays.

**How the orchestrator uses this:** in Phase 1, pick 3 seeds that span *different
engines* (not three pairs from the same engine). Diversity of engine is what keeps
the idea pool from collapsing into one shape. Each seed lists a one-line thesis
about why that intersection or shift is fertile ground.

*Theory: the best deep-tech ideas live in the overlap where neither field alone
sees the opportunity.*

- **Microbiome × Personalized medicine** — gut-brain axis diagnostics

## Engine B — Business-Model Innovation

*Theory: the product can be old; the way you package, price, or sell it is the
innovation. Most fintech and infra giants are this engine.*

- **Unbundling the incumbent** — take one painful feature out of a bloated suite
  and make it a focused product 10× better (Superhuman vs. Gmail, Ramp vs. the
  corporate card buried in an ERP).
- **"Stripe-for-X" infrastructure** — turn a regulated/complex capability into a
  developer API others build on (payments, payroll, insurance, lending, KYC).
- **Usage-based / outcome-based pricing** — charge per result instead of per seat
  in a market trained on seats; aligns price with value and undercuts incumbents.
- **Vertical SaaS + embedded fintech** — own the software for a niche industry,
  then attach payments/lending/insurance as the real margin (Toast, Procore).
- **Marketplace take-rate on an existing offline transaction** — a transaction
  already happens by phone/paper; move it online and take a clip (Faire, Flexport).
- **Buy-and-build / roll-up with software** — acquire fragmented offline operators
  and unify them with one software spine (the "software-enabled roll-up").
- **Financing as the wedge** — the financing (BNPL, equipment leasing, factoring)
  is the product; the software is the customer-acquisition cost.

## Engine C — Distribution / GTM Innovation

*Theory: the idea isn't new, but a new channel makes it winnable. Distribution is
the moat. Figma, Notion, and Calendly won on go-to-market, not on features.*

- **Embedded / PLG wedge** — ship an SDK or free tool that rides inside products
  customers already use; the integration is the distribution (Stripe, Plaid).
- **Bottoms-up land-and-expand** — a free self-serve tool one employee adopts,
  spreading to the team before sales ever calls (Figma, Linear).
- **Community-led / content-led** — the company is a community first and a product
  second; the audience is the unfair acquisition channel (HubSpot, dbt).
- **Creator / influencer distribution** — products built for and sold through the
  creator economy, where the creator *is* the channel.
- **Partner-led / integration-marketplace** — distribution comes from being the
  default app inside a platform's marketplace (Shopify apps, Salesforce AppExchange).
- **Bundling into an existing daily workflow** — insert the product where the user
  already is (browser, IDE, Slack, the POS) instead of asking them to come to you.
- **Reverse-trial / "free for the riskiest user"** — give away the part incumbents
  charge for to acquire the account, monetize the expansion.

## Engine D — Regulatory Arbitrage & Policy Shifts

*Theory: a new law, license, or deregulation suddenly creates (or unlocks) a
market. The "why now" is the statute. Open banking made Plaid; telehealth waivers
made Ro and Hims.*

- **Open-banking / open-finance mandates** — regulation forces incumbents to expose
  data via API; build the rails or the apps on top.
- **Right-to-repair laws** — new obligation to provide parts/manuals creates a
  marketplace and tooling opportunity around independent repair.
- **Carbon / ESG disclosure rules** — mandatory reporting (CSRD, SEC climate rules)
  creates demand for measurement, auditing, and offset-verification software.
- **Telehealth & licensing-compact expansion** — relaxed cross-state practice rules
  unlock new care-delivery and provider-network models.
- **Cannabis / psychedelics legalization** — newly legal markets need banking,
  compliance, seed-to-sale tracking, and commerce infrastructure from scratch.
- **Privacy regimes (GDPR/CPRA) as a wedge** — compliance becomes a recurring,
  non-optional spend; build the consent, deletion, and audit infrastructure.
- **Government procurement / defense-tech openings** — new authorities and budgets
  (e.g. dual-use, FedRAMP fast-tracks) open a buyer that was previously closed.

## Engine E — Community / Network Effects

*Theory: value increases with each user, so the network is the product and the
moat. Hard to start (cold-start problem) but durable once lit.*

- **Prosumer tool with a built-in network** — a tool individuals love that gets
  better as their collaborators join (Figma multiplayer, Notion sharing).
- **Data co-op / benchmarking network** — members contribute data, everyone gets
  aggregated insight no one could compute alone (salary, pricing, threat-intel).
- **Two-sided niche marketplace with density** — pick a market narrow enough to
  reach liquidity, then expand (vertical labor, equipment, specialty supply).
- **Single-player-utility → multiplayer-network** — start with standalone value so
  there's no cold-start, then layer the network on top (Superhuman → shared, dbt).
- **Professional / identity network for an underserved trade** — the LinkedIn for a
  vertical that LinkedIn serves poorly (nurses, truckers, tradespeople).
- **Open-source community → commercial layer** — the community builds the gravity;
  the company sells the hosting, governance, or enterprise tier.

## Engine F — Behavior-Change & Cultural Shifts

*Theory: how people live and work changed, and the old products don't fit the new
behavior. Remote work made Zoom/Notion; the solo economy made Stripe Atlas/Mercury.*

- **Remote / distributed work** — async-first tools, virtual HQs, global payroll
  and the long tail of "the office used to do this for me."
- **Solo economy / one-person business** — the rise of solo founders and creators
  needs back-office (banking, incorporation, accounting, ops) that assumes no team.
- **Longevity & proactive health** — a cultural shift from sick-care to optimization
  (continuous metrics, preventive testing, healthspan coaching).
- **Post-AI workflows** — work where the human is now an editor/orchestrator of AI
  output; build the tools, review layers, and trust infrastructure that implies.
- **Trust / authenticity backlash** — as AI floods channels with synthetic content,
  demand grows for provenance, verification, and human-proof.
- **Climate-adapted living** — insurance retreat, heat, water, and resilience are
  reshaping where and how people live; build for the adaptation, not just mitigation.
- **Aging population & care** — the demographic inversion creates demand for care
  coordination, caregiver tools, and age-tech that the current system can't staff.
