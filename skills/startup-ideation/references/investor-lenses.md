# Investor Lenses

The shared toolkit of judgment for this skill. The **Champion** (Phase 2) uses
these to build the strongest case for each idea; the **Assassin** (Phase 2) uses
them to find the fatal flaw; **Synthesizer-Prime** (Phase 4) uses them to score.
Each lens is a named perspective with one sharp question. Apply them by name —
"Under the Thiel lens…" — so the reasoning is legible.

---

## The Thiel Lens — Contrarian Monopoly (the Secret)

> "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"

A great startup is built on a **secret**: something true and valuable that the
consensus has missed. Two failure modes to screen for:
- **No secret** — everyone already agrees this is a good idea. Then it's either
  already being done or it's not actually an opportunity. Consensus ≠ opportunity.
- **A false secret** — a contrarian belief that's contrarian because it's *wrong*.

Then ask the monopoly question: **could this become a monopoly in a small market
first?** Start by dominating a niche so small incumbents ignore it, then expand.
A business that competes on price in a crowded market has no secret and no moat.

**Score signal:** high Secret & Novelty requires a *non-consensus* insight, not
merely a new feature. "New" is not the same as "a secret."

## The YC / Paul Graham Lens — Make Something People Want

> "Who *desperately* wants this right now?"

- **Painkiller vs. vitamin** — does this solve a hair-on-fire problem, or is it a
  nice-to-have someone would adopt if they had spare time? Vitamins die in the gap
  between "interesting" and "I'll pay for it."
- **Talk to users** — is there evidence real people have this problem *today*, or
  is the demand assumed? The best founders have watched users suffer the problem.
- **Make something a few people love, not something many people like** — narrow and
  intense beats broad and lukewarm. Where's the intensity?

**Score signal:** if you can't name a specific person who would be upset if this
product disappeared, viability is low regardless of how clever the tech is.

## The Christensen Lens — Jobs To Be Done

> "What job is the customer hiring this product to do?"

People don't buy products; they hire them to make progress in a situation. Frame
the idea as the *job*, not the technology. This reveals the true competitor (often
"do nothing" or a spreadsheet) and the real basis of differentiation. An idea with
no clearly articulated job is a solution looking for a problem.

## The Caldwell Lens — Tarpit Avoidance

> "Try to go more off the beaten path — from your personal experience, from
> diversifying your information diet." — Dalton Caldwell

A **tarpit idea** is one that looks attractive to many founders, attracts hundreds
of attempts, and almost always traps them — because the reason it hasn't been done
is non-obvious and fatal, not because no one thought of it. Classic tarpits:
- Consumer social / "an app to meet people / make friends"
- "Uber for X" with no path to local density or liquidity
- Marketplaces with no defensible take-rate (buyers and sellers disintermediate)
- Ad-funded niche content / media with no distribution edge
- "A better email / to-do / notes app" with no wedge
- Coordination apps for groups (events, splitting bills) that die on cold-start
- B2B2C plays where you control neither the business nor the consumer

The tell of a tarpit: *many smart people have tried, the idea still sounds good,
and yet none succeeded.* If an idea is a tarpit, the burden is to name the
specific reason this attempt escapes the trap. No escape story → it's a tarpit.

Caldwell's second point — **diversify your information diet** — is why this skill
runs multiple idea *engines* (see `idea-engines.md`): the best ideas come from
inputs other founders aren't looking at.

## The Hoover Lens — Why Now

> "What new thing can you build today that couldn't be built yesterday?"
> — Ryan Hoover

Every strong idea has a **why-now**: a specific, recent shift that opened the
window. Three kinds:
- **Technology shift** — a new capability, API, price drop, or hardware threshold
  (a model, a sensor cost, an open protocol). *Name the specific change, not "AI is
  getting better."*
- **Behavior shift** — people now do something they didn't (remote work, solo
  business, short-form video, proactive health).
- **Regulation shift** — a new law, license, or mandate created or unlocked a market
  (see Engine D in `idea-engines.md`).

**Score signal:** a vague or missing why-now is a yellow flag — either the idea was
buildable years ago (so why isn't it done?) or the window hasn't opened yet (too
early). The best why-now is recent (last 1–3 years) and specific.

---

## How the lenses combine

A truly strong idea tends to clear all five: a **secret** (Thiel) that powers a
**painkiller** (YC) doing a clear **job** (Christensen), that **isn't a tarpit**
(Caldwell), opened by a real **why-now** (Hoover). An idea that fails any one of
these has a named, specific weakness — which is exactly what the Champion must
defend and the Assassin must exploit.
